Chapter One · The Machine

A story told in moments and marbles.

The assignment sounded simple. Build something that raises a flag, and make every step part of a story. The catch was a single rule: once it starts, no one may touch the table. To get the flag up, twenty-one little contraptions had to cooperate in order, each one quietly setting off the next.

We told Charlie and the Chocolate Factory with ours, from Charlie tearing open the Wonka bar all the way to the flag rising as he inherits the whole factory.

Every step obeys one rule

Trigger→Action→Output→next Trigger
Where this is going

Nineteen of those steps learned to hand off on their own. Two did not, and those two are where we did our best thinking. We will walk through both, one at a time.

The TwoInterventions

Two places where the machine stalled and needed a human hand. Tap one to jump straight in, or begin with the first.

Intervention 1 of 2

The piece that would not fall.

Step 16. The marble met the orange road piece, and it just stood there.

The marble came down the winding track and tapped the orange piece right where we had planned. The piece gave a small rock, settled back upright, and the whole Wonka tour stalled there at the Nut Room. It clearly arrived with something to give. It simply could not turn that into enough rotation to carry the piece over the edge it was balancing on.

Tipping needs a turning effort, a moment, larger than the one gravity is using to hold the piece up. A moment grows the higher up you push, so where the marble hit mattered as much as how hard.

  • 1It arrived slow.Every bend in the winding track quietly bled off speed, so the marble reached the piece gentler than we had pictured.
  • 2It struck low.A push near the base has almost no leverage, so most of its energy tried to slide the piece sideways instead of rotating it.
  • 3The piece was stable.A wide base and a low center of mass meant it needed a real lift to swing over the edge.

Hit higher, start it leaning, and protect the speed.

  • 1Strike high on a paddle.The same push does far more tipping near the top of the piece.
  • 2Pre-lean it on a rail.Set its center of mass already hovering over the edge, so a gentle tap finishes the job.
  • 3Protect the speed.Shorten and straighten the last run of track, and use a heavier, denser ball.
  • 4Remove the guesswork.A short guide channel lands the marble at the same spot, at the same angle, every time.

Intervention 2 of 2

The blow that did not land.

Near the end. The final hammer swung down, and the launcher never fired.

Twenty steps had behaved. The flag was sitting right there, waiting. The hammer came down, met the trigger, and nothing happened. A miss this close to the finish is rarely the fault of the last step alone, and this one was no exception.

A swinging hammer has plenty of energy. The real trouble was that almost none of it reached the trigger in a form the trigger could actually use.

  • 1It hit off-axis.A hammer on a hinge swings through an arc, so only part of its force pushed the way the button could move.
  • 2The hinge had friction.Friction we never measured skimmed a little speed off every swing.
  • 3The button was stiff.It needed a full, firm press, and a quick graze never finished it.
  • 4Tolerances stacked up.Twenty tiny errors upstream had quietly walked the target away from where the hammer expected it.

Trip a catch instead of pressing a button, and stop the target from drifting.

  • 1Trip a light catch.Pre-load the launcher so the hammer only has to knock a catch free.
  • 2Add a lever or cam.Turn the swing into exactly the direction and travel the release needs.
  • 3Guide it, then stop it.A guide and a hard stop land the hammer on the same spot, on a contact plate larger than the drift.
  • 4Build in margin.Design it with more force than the bare minimum, a real factor of safety.

Chapter Four · Building it better

The hard part was doing it twenty-one times in a row.

A twenty-one step machine is a chain. Every step leans on the one before it, so the chances all multiply together. Drag the slider and watch what that does.

If each step works 95% of the time…

…the whole machine works only

34%

twenty-one steps, multiplied together

What we would do differently

The fix is mostly unglamorous. We would give every step room to spare, set things up the same way each time, and test each part on its own, many times over, before ever trusting it in the chain.

Chapter Five · The Final Exam

Every lesson was hiding in the machine.

We did not always notice, but every topic from the board had been built into the build. Tap one to see where it turned up.

Found in steps 6 · 9 · 14 · 16

The rotating wooden levers are moment balances about a pivot, the very diagrams we drew over and over. The orange piece that would not fall was a moment problem we lost, and working out exactly why is our most honest proof the lesson landed.

Found in steps 12 · 16

The marble that rolls off the box and the piece that would not tip are both, underneath, the same question: where does a center of mass sit relative to the edge it leans on.

Found in steps 1 · 8 · 13 · 15

Why a heavier ball changes everything, why a higher ramp gives more speed, and why a winding track quietly takes it back. The whole machine ran on energy turning into motion, and motion setting off the next step.

Found in steps 4 · 5 · 19 · 20

The ramps are inclined planes, the hammer is a lever, and the blade that cuts the rubber band at the climax is a wedge, doing exactly the job a wedge was born to do.

Found in steps 7 · 15 · 19

The levers, the winding road, and the launcher all take motion in one form and hand it back in another, which is the whole reason a chain of steps can exist at all.

Found in Revision 01 · Revision 02

We learned what a factor of safety is for by going without one, and what tolerance stack-up means by watching twenty harmless little errors add up to one launcher that would not fire.

Found across the whole build

Dependencies, milestones spread across the year, and a critical path where one weak step halts everything after it. You handed us a project that could only be finished the way real engineering gets finished.

Chapter Six · Looking Back

Two years,three machines.

Now that this year’s mechanical engineering course is finished, we wanted to step outside the Rube Goldberg project and look back. Two earlier favorites taught us the very same lesson.

A classmate’s invention

Let the shape do the sorting.

Two panels that slowly spread apart, so the gap between them widens along their length. Small balls drop through first, large ones hold on and fall later, and nothing decides this but the geometry. No sensors, no motors.

The clever part is how little it has to do. The logic lives in the shape itself, so the physics quietly handles the work. The hard part was the tolerancing, the very same instinct that would have saved our launcher.

A classmate’s invention

Aim beats amount.

Curve the foil so that every parallel ray of sunlight bends toward one point. Light that was doing nothing spread flat across a panel becomes a spot hot enough to cook.

The sunlight was never the hard part, since it had been falling on everything all day. The whole trick was concentration: get the curve right, then place whatever you want to heat exactly where all that light agrees to meet.

The thread through all three

The same lesson, in three accents.

oven: focus on the target
sorter: gap at the right width
our machine: aim, not just force
→
one idea

Line the three up and they say the same thing. The oven works when the focus lands on the target. The sorter works when the gap opens at the right width. And both of our failures came from the other side of it: the marble had real momentum but spent it striking low, and the hammer had real energy but spent it striking off-axis.

A little energy aimed exactly where you need it beats a lot of energy sprayed almost everywhere.

Chapter Nine · The Chocolatiers

The most important step was never on the table.

We can draw a falling hammer. We cannot really draw the thing that built this machine, which was seven people refusing to let it stay broken: the late nights, the kitchens that smelled like hot glue, the marble that misbehaved at the worst possible moment, and the ridiculous joy when a run finally went clean and somebody cheered out loud.

You wrote a rule that the step before yours and the step after yours had to be built by someone else. It took us all year to see why. None of us could ever finish alone. The shape of the machine, where each result becomes the next person’s start, turned out to be a quiet picture of our own team.

Trigger, action, output was how the machine ran, and somewhere along the way it became how the seven of us ran too.

One more flag,for you.

You gave this academy eleven years, and twenty-six before that as an engineer out in industry. Somewhere in all of it you decided to teach us with a workbench, a story, and a problem that could only be solved slowly and honestly. We did not always realize you were teaching us something bigger than statics. We do now.

Our machine was built to raise a flag, and after two honest failures and a great deal of midnight, it did. We would like to raise one more, by hand this time, and leave it up.

Thank you for the pearls of engineering wisdom, for the red marks that quietly made us better, and for taking a group of teenagers seriously as engineers long enough that we started to become them.

With every thank you we have,
